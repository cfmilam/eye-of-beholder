Preface

After the launch of the POAMS (Pope-Osborne Angular Momentum Synthesis) website www.poams.org in 2003, a series of lectures and seminars was arranged. The many interesting and sometimes taxing questions which arose as a consequence of these meetings have led to the production of this monograph. The author wishes to thank Penny Gower, for her hard work and support in organising the meetings. Thanks are due also to those people, few of whom are qualified in physics or philosophy, who nevertheless posed the penetrating commonsense questions that have prompted him to produce this work.

One of the main aims of this text will be to present some very intricate arguments in the plainest possible language, without recourse to either the customary in-group scientific and philosophical jargon or to any condescending attempt at the 'dumbing down' of abstruse professional arguments. The level aimed at is that of ordinary commonsense intelligence, which we rate more highly than is customary in circles of professional science. In other words, the aim of this monograph is, as it were, to put the claims of modern theorists before a jury of ordinary, sane and intelligent non-specialists, the sorts of people who are usually entrusted, in even the most difficult of instances, to judge the merits or demerits of a case. It is also pointed out that although some mathematical formulae have had to be included in these pages, these equations are very simple, and that while an understanding of the argument may be enhanced by a knowledge of mathematics, these mathematical details need not prevent non-mathematicians from judging the argument as a whole on the basis of pure commonsense logic.

This argument will be that certain trends in modern physics reveal that the original scientific conception of nature as a great and determinate machine is now gradually giving way to a view of nature as a vast, interactive, informational matrix, or hologram. As might be expected, this will require radical revisions in some of our most basic and all-too-familiar, traditional ways of thinking about nature. Not least, it should point the need for us, as logically minded custodians of commonsense, to keep a watchful eye on the theoretical excesses of our scientists.

I. Introduction

There are many sciences and scientific theories but there is only one nature. Why, then, during our Science's more than two thousand years of evolution, have its representations of nature become so increasingly fragmented? Something must be wrong, and seriously wrong, somewhere.

Explanations may abound as to just what it is that has gone wrong. In these pages we leave such explanations to the historical analyst and ask ourselves a more fundamental question: What is the most basic, most commonly acceptable definition of a science of nature that is possible? And what else can that most basic definition of science be but that it is the rational interpretation of observational and instrumental information? And what, other than ourselves, both directly and in communication with others like ourselves, is the ultimate reader of that information? Right at its very start, therefore, science is relativistic, which is to say that whatever it describes as existing in nature is described and measured relatively to observation.

Right at that same relativistic root lies also the fact that scientific information is quantised. This follows from the fact that for information to be information, its ultimate elements, or informational bits, have to be discrete and unconnected. If there were laws determining what letters of the alphabet, or sounds in speech 'caused' them to appear next to one another in this sentence, all sentences would be the same as this one and there would be no such thing as news. That is to say, to be in any way informative, these sequences of informational elements have to be probabilistic, not determinate. In the jargon of Information Technology, they have to be stochastic [from the Greek stokhazesthai, to aim at, or conjecture (stokhos meaning a target)].

And indeed, this is precisely what we find on the most basic level of our contacts with nature, where information, in all sectors of observation, is quantised in this way into discrete events of this very same indeterminate or probabilistic nature. Each such event is a complete and irreducible packet, or quantum, of energy times time, or action, in units of Planck's constant $h$.

But if we, as observers, are the readers of this information, then what are its 'writers'? What else can these be but those objects whose informational 'bar-codes' we read with instruments such as the spectrometer. Together with these and other instruments such as the telescope and microscope we can extrapolate, from the information presented in observation â€” that is, from natural phenomena â€” all that we know about the distances and directions of objects, their chemical characteristics, their positions and motions in relation to ourselves and to one another, their relative masses â€” in fact, everything that is known, in science, about what we perceive as physical objects and processes.

But of course, all physical events involve time. What is time? Metaphysics aside, what else can time be but what we read with those instruments we call clocks? Some of those objects we extrapolate from observational information are clocks. A clock, in this sense, is a physical process which we arbitrarily choose as a time-standard by which to measure the rates of other processes. Examples of such standards are, historically, the period of the earth's daily rotation or that of the moon around the earth, the earth around the sun, and so on. Moreover, we have regulated mechanisms of the sort that John Harrison constructed in the 18th century, for the express purpose of fixing an arbitrary non-local time-standard such as Greenwich Mean Time for purposes of coordinating railway timetables and plotting the positions of ships at sea. This notion of a 'global time', or time that is the same everywhere began with the classical scientists, Galileo and Newton. Not only was this time conceived to be the same all over the earth, it was also assumed to extend into the whole universe of matter and space. This universalised or 'absolute' time would be the time ticked by an ideal clock, constructed along Harrison lines, to maintain the same steady rate of ticks regardless of wherever it went in the universe or of how it got there. This observationally unjustified assumption of an underlying absolute, overall time was doomed, however, not to survive the advance of scientific knowledge, for the following reasons.

A very good clock, or regular periodic process, is that of the eclipses of Jupiter's principal satellite, Io, as it orbits its parent planet. Olaus RÃ¶mer, the Danish astronomer, in 1676, was the first to observe that as this clock moves away from the terrestrial observer it loses time and that as it moves towards the observer it gains time. This ratio of distance-change to time-change RÃ¶mer found to be a constant, $c$, the refined value of which is now 299,792,458 metres to the second.

It is at this point in our scientific history, with RÃ¶mer's discovery of $c$, that classical physics approaches its conceptual 'sell-by date' and modern theoretical physics, for better or worse, begins to take over. The emphasis, here, falls on the word 'theoretical', as applied to a realm of speculative objects and processes that are never directly observed nor are even observable except in what are conceived to be their effects on things actually observed. The first move in this theoretical direction, on the grounds that $c$ has the dimensions of a velocity, was to assume that it is a velocity. But a velocity of what? In this way there immediately arises the theoretical question of what it is, exactly, that is supposed to be moving at this 'velocity $c$'. And, as everyone knows, what was assumed to be moving was 'light', conceived as a travelling intermediary, the conveyor, or retailer of the information by which we observe things.

However, conceived in this way, 'light' is purely theoretical. We never see light leave an object and wing its way towards us in order for us to see the object. Nor do we ever see light travelling between one object and another in a pure vacuum, far less in any 'luminiferous ether'. What is commonly regarded, therefore, as the 'fact' that light travels in any sense is not a fact at all but, in the last analysis, just pure supposition.

It is from this pure supposition that practically all our modern theories stem as to what it is that is travelling in that completely invisible way and by what invisible means. The quantised character of the observed spectral energies suggests particles, whereas their periodic and resonant character suggests waves. But waves in what? What is there to wave in a vacuum? And what sorts of 'particles' are there that can possibly behave like waves in experimental situations?

Theories abound, therefore, as to what it is that conducts these luminous energies from one place to another. Is it some invisible quasi-substance such as a 'wave-particle' â€” whatever that might be? Do the 'waves' travel in some sort of 'field', or 'ether'? Speculative theories still vie with one another as to what might be the defining properties and characteristics of this assumed light-conveying 'medium'. But despite all attempts to detect and measure these properties, even with the very best instruments that modern technology can devise, empirical science has drawn a blank. In modern physics, all conception of the information we call light as consisting of waves and/or particles, either in vacuo or in some kind of medium, have ended, as they began, as bits of pure theoretical speculation.

However, apart from any question of the sheer pragmatical value of this plethora of working-theories in the development of our scientific history, it is a plain fact that this interpretation of $c$ as a 'velocity', which led to the whole theoretical extravaganza, was never logically obligatory. For instance, it does not automatically follow that because all velocities are distances divided by time, all distances divided by time are velocities. I can divide the length of my lawn by the time since the Kennedy assassination, without that signifying any kind of 'velocity'.

The essential difference is that velocity truly applies to something that is definitely travelling, which, as we have seen, is eminently not the case with $c$. The logical alternative to interpreting $c$ as a 'velocity' is as described by Herman Bondi in his book Assumption and Myth in Physical Theory:1

> Any attempt to measure the velocity of light is. . . not an attempt at measuring the velocity of light but an attempt at ascertaining the length of the standard metre in Paris in terms of time-units.

This logical alternative to the historical interpretation of $c$ as a 'velocity' marks the point of departure for the whole new ahistorical approach to physics described in this book, an approach which, although it has been around for centuries in the name of 'phenomenalism', has been scarcely more than just hinted at in the chronicles of scientific philosophy. In this alternative approach, the traditional model of the universe as a Great Machine, whose ultimate parts are self-sufficient atoms separated by a self-sufficient vacuum and causally connected by an incidental travelling intermediary called 'light', is replaced by a very different model. This is the model of an informational system in which light, far from being some incidental travelling intermediary between atoms in space, is essentially what is observed in all its direct and instrumental manifestations from below infrared to beyond ultraviolet and gamma. Light, in this all-inclusive sense is therefore the fount of all the information from which all properties and dimensions of objects, of space, time and whatever, are projected â€” even what we call (or, rather, miscall) the 'velocity of light'. This is the model of an informational system which, in this book, for want of any better description we refer to as a 'hologram'. The distinguishing features and implications of this radically divergent, holistic or 'holographic' approach to physics that is logically implied by the Bondian re-interpretation of $c$ are as described in the following pages.

II. The Universal 'Hologram'

The word 'hologram', as used in these pages, does not signify, as in the usual, technological language, a 'plate which gives rise to 3D images'. It signifies more of a philosophical concept, in which the emphasis falls on "holo-", meaning whole and in which the dimensions of the optical images are dependent upon the manner in which optical information is observed.

Now in light, taking its full spectral range from deepest infrared to farthest ultraviolet and Gamma, is contained all the information there is about the world and our place in it. Even in what, to us are the very darkest of places there is light, albeit light that we, as human observers, can never see. This invisible light consists, fundamentally, of the information that atoms transact with one another, which interrelates them, signalling to each other their relative existence and dispositions in ways well below and above the thresholds of ordinary optical sensation.

This is no problem because what we cannot observe with our eyes we may observe with our other senses, such as those of touch, taste, smell and so on. Moreover, in all these different categories of human sensation there are instruments which communicate to our senses what they detect in all their various non-human ways. What we cannot see with our eyes we may therefore perceive in all those ways known to natural science, all of which are based, ultimately, on the same sort of information that we now understand by 'light'.

In this all-inclusive sense, then, the word 'light' loses its customary meaning as something ancillary to our knowledge of the world. Its meaning expands to become coextensive with that of the world itself, the matrix of experience into which all scientific knowledge analyses without remainder. In other words, in light as we now understand it â€” that is, as something directly observed or detected â€” we have all the information that is both necessary and sufficient for the conduct of science.

What we do not have in light is any direct or instrumental information, in any category of sensation or instrumentation, as to what we suppose is the existence and movement of the light in those voids between objects (ultimately, atoms) that we extrapolate from the information contained in the light. Indeed, there is a profound fallacy in supposing that we can ever have information about anything underlying or conveying that information which is not, in itself, information of the same kind.

This means that there can be none of those gaps, voids or spaces that we ordinarily think of as separating the ultimate informational bits out of which all space and time are extrapolated. All such gaps or voids are observational projections out of the information. They cannot also be preconditions for the information out of which they are projected.

This reverses the traditional priority of conception which puts light in space by putting space â€” and time, and everything â€” in the light. There is then no question of light 'travelling at the rate $c$ in vacuo'. The ratio $c$ is simply a dimensional constant in Bondi's sense of a 'conversion factor' for converting our observationally projected measures in conventional metres into conventional measures in seconds, in the way that one might convert measures in pounds into measures in kilograms, or measures in yards into measures in metres. But of course, it is easy to see how one set of units of a measure such as length or weight can be converted into different units of the same measure. It is by no means so easy to see how units of one measure, such as length, can be converted into units of a different measure, such as time. This is because we are long habituated to thinking of length and time as different and independent kinds of measure. We 'naturally' assume that when we are measuring length we are not measuring time, and vice versa. But nature tells us that this assumption was just plain wrong. There never was any distance, in and of itself, nor any time in and of itself. This was simply an illusion, due to the fact that until RÃ¶mer's astronomical discovery (see above) mankind had never measured distances on such a scale as to reveal that all observational distances are also observational times. The eventual discovery was, in its simplest, non-theoretical terms, that distance was never pure distance, as traditionally imagined, but was always, if only we could have been aware of it, distance-time. In short, from that moment on, what our science has had to adapt to has been the fact that there is no such thing as a pure distance in metres but only an observational distance in which the traditionally distinct measures in metres and measures in seconds are inseparable.

In all of this, of course, the stress falls on direct observation. This is fundamentally opposed to the 'God's-eye-view' of an absolute space and an absolute time underlying everything, all direct knowledge of which is impossible except via light, the incidental travelling intermediary between things and our knowledge of them.

II.1 The Relativity of Time

A curious logical consequence of this merging of observational distance and time is that objects which travel in space also travel in time. There is a sense in which this statement is obvious, since travelling any distance always takes some measure of time. (No-one has ever travelled from A to B instantly.) However, to say that distance is time is to say that instead of bodies being observed to travel pure distances in metres per second of time, as previously imagined, they travel observational distances in seconds of time per observational second of time. In other words, every $3 \times 10^8$ metres$/c$ that a body travels relatively to the observer is one second of time, regardless of how fast or how slowly it travels.

This means that if $t$ is the standard period of a clock when stationary relative to the observer (as seen in the observer's telescope, say) then if, in that standard time $t$, the clock is seen to travel either away from or towards the observer over a distance $s$, the observational resultant $t_o$ of those dimensional components, $t$ and $s/c$ will be (by Pythagoras),2

$$t_o = \sqrt{t^2 + s^2/c^2}$$

Now since the relative or observational velocity of the object is $v = s/t_o$, then substituting for $s$ in (1) the equivalent expression $vt_o$ produces

$$t_o = \sqrt{(vt_o/c)^2 + t^2}$$

which simplifies directly to the familiar Einsteinian time-dilational formula

$$t_o = t\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}$$

It will no doubt be noticed that this equivalent of the Einsteinian formula is derived in a manner which is entirely independent of the usual historical way.3 That is to say, it is derived not only with the utmost simplicity, as opposed to the notoriously abstruse rationale of Einsteinian relativity that is taught to students but also â€” and most significantly â€” without any recourse to the second axiom of Einstein's Special Theory of Relativity, which states that light travels in a vacuum at the constant speed $c$.

II.2 Instantaneous time-delay

These relativistic formulae are completely pristine and radical â€” just how much so may be seen in due course. First, however, we note that from formula (1) it follows that what has been traditionally conceived as a delayed time $s/c$ between one light-transacting body and another is intrinsically instantaneous (i.e., when, in this equation, the 'travelling time' $t_o$ is $s/c$, the proper time, $t$ of the transition is zero).

Many people find this extremely puzzling. It seems a complete contradiction to say that light which is proven to take time to travel in space between a transmitting source and a receiver also takes no time at all. Certainly, in any ordinary way of thinking, to say that an action which is time-delayed is also instantaneous seems like a logical contradiction. And so it is â€” if applied to the same single action. Logically, however, an action may have both time-delayed and instantaneous components. On a TV screen, for instance, what the viewer sees as a straightforward mechanical motion from A to B of an object in the scenario is really no such thing. We know very well that these apparently smooth and continuous motions analyse-out into time-delayed processions of instantaneous and sporadic pixel events, and that it is from the informational patterns and sequences of these events that the viewer projects all the length, breadth, depth and activity of the video scenario. Anyone who is familiar with this technology can appreciate how absurd it would be to project into any one of those pixel-events the same sort of beginning and end in the space of the scenario that actions may have in that scenario. For instance, we know that the sequences of pixel-events which the viewer projects, such as a bullet fired from a gun and travelling across the screen to strike a target, do not have the same sort of velocity, momentum and other mechanical characteristics that the viewer ascribes to the bullet.

The conception to aim at, then, in order to understand this revolutionary paradigm shift, is to switch from thinking of the ultimate informational quanta as travelling through space in the form of bullet-like light-corpuscles or as waves, to thinking of them as the irreducible observational elements out of informational patterns and sequences of which all that we perceive as space, time and motion are 'holographically' projected. The demand this makes on our powers of imagination is that of i) setting aside, clearly and consistently, the habit of assuming that nature is primarily mechanical and only kinematical in our observational representations of it (in films, videos, etc.,) and ii) adopting instead an alternative view of nature as being primarily kinematical and mechanical only in our observational representations of it. In the language of philosophy, this is to cancel the classical idea that mechanics is a priori and kinematics a posteriori, and, instead, to make kinematics a priori and mechanics a posteriori â€” a complete conceptual turnaround.

In this literally revolutionary way of thinking, which is on a par with Copernicus' reversing of the classical notion of the sun going round the earth to the earth going round the sun, we completely invert the classical notion of light being in space to space being in light.4 In all the discussions on which this present book is based, analysis reveals that the biggest sources of confusion have been due to the intrinsic intellectual difficulty of keeping these two completely incompatible ways of thinking clearly and consistently apart. To adopt the revolutionary view is to see that at the basic level of observational information which we customarily call light, there is nothing taking place between objects and observers of the sort that we normally conceive as 'interaction' involving mechanical corpuscles ('photons') travelling in a primordial vacuum, waves travelling in 'ethers', 'fields' or whatever. All that theoretical paraphernalia is removed in favour of a straightforward, non-theoretical, purely observer-based, or phenomenalist, approach to physics in which the ultimate informational elements (quanta) are as much an integral part of an object as of one's observation of it. All the usual basic dimensions of space, time, mass, etc. â€” including those of so-called 'light-velocity' â€” are then no more nor less than informational extrapolations made by the decoder (the observer) from directly observed patterns and sequences of the informational bits. This is in the same way that all the breadth, depth and duration of a video scenario are projected by the viewer out of patterns and sequences of informational elements. In the video case, these elements are the sporadically lighted screen pixels; in the real case they are the quanta into which all physical action ultimately analyses.

III. A Unified Non-Field Theory

When we look into space, at the planets, stars and so on, what do we see? We see objects separated from one another in different directions and dimensions. What we do not see is light 'travelling in vacuo' from these objects towards us or towards one another. Nor do we see the 'gravitational force' that is supposed to pull these objects together into orbital configurations like those of the solar system. What we actually see are bodies freely orbiting one another in systems of angular momentum. This is the observational quantity $mvr$, where $m$ is the mass of the orbiting body, $v$ is its orbital velocity and $r$ is the radius of orbit â€” conceived, here, as ideally circular, for simplicity.

All this is a matter of plain perception, these parameters $m$, $v$ and $r$ being projected into the physical scenario from direct observational information in the way that has already been described. In historical hindsight, then, it seems strange that Isaac Newton, viewing the motions of the heavens, conceived it as a "Law of Nature' that natural force-free motion takes place in straight lines. Newton's so-called 'First Law of Motion' is therefore profoundly unempirical, since nowhere can bodies be observed to travel in accordance with that 'Law'. Nothing in space travels in that way. Even balls on billiard tables and pucks on ice would be seen to travel in curves if the surfaces on which they travel were long enough.

Nowhere, then, is there any truly straight-line momentum $mv$ of the sort that Newton envisaged. All momentum is angular momentum $mvr$ according to the equation

$$mvr = GmM/v \qquad (4)$$

where $m$ is the orbiting mass, $M$ is the balancing countermass, $v$ and $r$ are the velocity and radius, respectively of the (ideally circular) orbit, and $G$ is an empirical factor for ordinary free moving and non-spinning bodies.

What's new, here, is not this angular momentum equation, which is a standard Newtonian formulation, but that this equation has been shown to be sufficient in itself to define the parameters of free-orbiting bodies without resorting to Newton's conception of an in vacuo 'gravitational force' to account for the curvilinear nature of those orbits.7

Newton's linear momentum is thus an ideal special case of an angular momentum with $r$ at theoretical infinity â€” a straight line and a circle of infinite radius being one and the same. When I drive my car, it stays on the road rather than travelling through the air because it does not have sufficient angular momentum to become a satellite. If it had, it would orbit the earth as naturally as the moon does and as the earth and moon do, together, around the sun. As it so happens, at whatever speed it is possible for my car to do, its natural force-free orbit with regard to the earth (if we imagine the earth's mass to be confined within the volume of, say, a football) would be somewhere way down below where we ordinarily stand.8 However, since the earth's surface normally prevents the car from following that natural force-free orbit, it presses upwards on the wheels of the car with a reactionary force of 9.78 newtons of force for every kilogram of the car's mass.

Why, then, did Newton's apple fall to earth in the way it did? It was because it did not have sufficient angular momentum to orbit the earth in the way the moon and other satellites do. Take away the moon's angular momentum with respect to the earth and the two bodies would automatically collapse together. This would not be by some invisible 'force of gravitational attraction', as our traditional textbooks describe it, but simply by a loss of the angular momentum which normally keeps bodies apart. By that same token, Newton's apple fell because with the angular momentum it possessed due to the earth's rotation at Woolsthorpe, its natural force-free orbit would have been 289/290ths of the distance from the ground under his feet to the earth's centre.9 The only force it makes sense to speak of, therefore, is not the traditional invisible 'force' acting in vacuo but the real, sensible and measurable force that is exerted by whatever it is that prevents the object from orbiting freely. In the case of Newton's apple this restraining object was the twig on which the apple hung before falling.

None of this conflicts with what, in commonsense contexts, we feel as the force preventing us from falling and which we measure on a weighing-scale or weighbridge as the 'force of gravity'. What it dispenses with is the theoretical notion of a 'gravitational field' surrounding bits of matter and pervading space. Indeed, it makes redundant all the other sorts of 'field' conceptions such as 'electric fields', 'magnetic fields' and so on, which have been constructed on the same theoretical (Newtonian) lines. For instance, converting the so-called 'charge' on an electron, in units of coulombs ($1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C) into a mechanical spin kinetic energy in joules ($2.18 \times 10^{-18}$ J), and taking the electron's mass ($9.109 \times 10^{-31}$ kg) as no more than just a pure mass, gives an angular momentum figure for that mass, of $mvr = h/2Ï€$ ($= 1.054 \times 10^{-34}$ kg mÂ²/s). These are precisely the parameters which Niels Bohr deduced for the electron in the hydrogen atom on the basis of classical 'electrodynamical' theory.10 In any event, the measurable force required to remove the 'electron' from its lowest, or ground-level orbit around the nucleus would be in the immensely larger order of $10^{20}$ times the force required for a pair of non-spinning masses equivalent to those of the so-called 'electron' and 'proton'. For these much tighter orbits the 'gravitational constant', which is normally $6.726 \times 10^{-11}$ NmÂ²/kg s for macrophysical bodies in ordinary astronomical and engineering situations, is hugely increased to $1.5 \times 10^{9}$ mÂ³/kg s for these particles with their far greater proportion of spin to orbital angular momentum. This is much more than would be possible for macrophysical bodies. Such macroscopic bodies would disintegrate long before such spin-energies could be reached, which is why, at the macrolevel, these spin-effects cannot normally be detected.

In this way, by replacing 'gravity' with angular momentum, the notorious problem of seeking the 'unified field' is solved automatically. This is by getting rid of these 'field' concepts altogether, along with all the theoretical paraphernalia that goes with them. Nor are these logical consequences to be judged trivial, or 'merely academic'. On the basis of this angular momentum synthesis it is predicted that a steel ball, of mass 2.5 kg and radius 5 cm, rotating at 2000 revolutions per second (it is just possible to spin such a ball at such a speed without disintegration) would increase or decrease in weight by one hundredth of a gram, depending on the direction of the spin with regard to that of the earth.11 So small is this predicted change in weight for such large amounts of spin that it is little wonder that it has never been noticed in ordinary macrophysical experience. Experiments to prove this predicted effect have not so far been carried out by our POAMS (Pope-Osborne Angular Momentum Synthesis) group for various reasons, not least due to a failure to obtain the necessary funding. However, comparing the POAMS predictions with the results of experiments that have been funded, such as those carried out by Hideo Hayasaka and his Japanese team at Tokai University,12 suggest that the changes in weight that they claimed for their disk of 0.175 kg, spinning at 18,000 rpm were not only much larger than we would have expected but were carried out in conditions not ideal for our prediction, which would be at the equator and therefore with an axis parallel (or anti-parallel) to that of the spinning earth. In the Hayasaka experiments, the disc was not dropped at or near the equator and was not spun in the same plane as the earth's rotation, so our theory predicts that the effects they recorded should have been even smaller than reported. This accords with the findings of Quinn and Picard of the International Bureau of Weights and Measures, who judged that the Hayasaka results were spurious.13

It should be mentioned, in passing, that ours is not the first attempt to dispose of Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation. As is well known, in his General Theory of Relativity, Einstein pointed out that the felt 'force' we call 'gravity' disappears when we are in free fall and that it can be reproduced in free space by standing on an accelerated surface such as the inside of a centrifuge or a rocket-propelled vehicle. This he called the 'principle of equivalence' between the felt force of acceleration and gravitational force. As a consequence, General Relativity geometrises the vacuum in such a way that when standing on the earth's surface we are being accelerated upwards at a rate of 32 feet or (9.75 metres) per second per second. Unfortunately for any chance of commonsense understanding, this is difficult to square with the rather obvious fact that the earth remains the same size and is not swelling at anything like that prodigious, exponential rate.

Moreover, General Relativity does not, in and of itself, explain the existence of the other in vacuo 'forces', such as electrostatic and magnetostatic force which were conceived by Newton's successors along the same lines as his 'universal gravitational force'. This left Einstein unable to solve the problem of the unification of the various field-forces, a problem which plagued him for the rest of his life and, in the end, defeated him. This implies no disrespect for Einstein's genius. Like the tasks that the gods of the Greek pantheon sometimes set for testing men, this task of solving the problem of the 'unified field' was not a real problem but an illusory one, hence impossible to solve. It was what the philosopher Wittgenstein would have called a 'Scheinproblem' â€” not a problem to be solved so much as dissolved.

IV. The Quantum-Informational 'Bar-Code'

In 1885, a Swiss secondary-school teacher, Johann Balmer, observed that the spectrum of light from a hydrogen gas forms what we would now call a 'bar-code-like' structure and he published a general mathematical formula for these lines. Then, in 1890, the Swedish physicist Johannes Rydberg extended Balmer's work and found a general rule applicable to many other elements. More series were discovered, both in the spectrum of hydrogen and in the spectra of other elements. Stated in terms of the frequency of the light, the generalised formula may be expressed as

$$\nu = N^2 cR \left[\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right] \tag{5}$$

Here, $\nu$ is frequency in cycles per second, $N$ is the ordinal number of the element ($N = 1$ for basic hydrogen), $cR$ is the Rydberg frequency 'constant' of the series (for a theoretically infinite nuclear mass) and $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the fixed and running terms of the series, respectively. It is to be stressed that this formula was obtained empirically, that is, simply by the trial-and-error method of picking numbers to fit the observed spectral lines. So no theory was involved at this particular stage as to why those numbers should arrange themselves in that way. The formula expresses no more than a fact of pure observation, as a spectral 'bar code' of information identifying the character of a substance.

IV.1 The historical route

But why do these bar-code-like patterns form in the way they do? It was not until 1913 that a theoretical explanation emerged. This was tendered by Niels Bohr, who based his explanation on a mixture of classical mechanics and electrodynamical theory, plus an ad hoc inclusion of the quantisation of action. This latter had been introduced in 1900 by Max Planck in his formulation of the distribution of the radiation emitted by a black body, or perfect absorber of radiant energy. The lines, Bohr supposed, were energies released by the elementary charged particles called 'electrons' orbiting like planets around the nuclear 'protons'. But, unlike the continuous motions of planets, these electrons changed orbits in discrete quantum jumps of energy $e = h\nu$, (where $\nu$ is the frequency of radiation and $h$ is Planck's constant). In Bohr's model, the jumps take place between orbit-levels denoted by the $n$ numbers in the above equation (i.e., $n = 1$ being the lowest orbit, or the one nearest the nucleus, and $n = 2, 3, 4,...$ etc. the outer orbit-levels in ascending order). The fixed term $n_1$ then designates the orbit-level from which the electron 'starts' its jump (nearest the nucleus) and the running term $n_2$ denotes the orbit-level at which it 'lands'. The atom thus loses (emits) or gains (absorbs) energy in whole and irreducible quantum amounts of Planck's constant $h$.

Modifications of this classical theory followed in quick succession. Finer structures of lines in the spectrum of hydrogen were discovered and these were explained by Arnold Sommerfeld in terms of elliptical orbits of the electrons. Then Pieter Zeeman showed that further spectral lines appeared when the sources of the spectrum were placed near a magnet. These newer orbital parameters were given newer quantum numbers, and these were rationalised by Bohr on the basis of three quantum numbers relating to the size, shape and direction of the electron's orbit. Transitions between these various orbital states sufficed to explain Sommerfeld's multiple lines, but they did not explain those extra lines that were produced by magnetism. These were eventually explained by Wolfgang Pauli as due to a hidden rotation, or spin, of the orbiting electron, and this same idea appeared in the work of the two Dutch physicists, George Uhlenbeck and Samuel Goudsmit.

With these and other theoretical modifications, the full informational contents of spectra were more or less adequately explained on the basis of the hidden motions of these invisible particles. Even the periodic table of the elements which the Russian chemist, Dimitri Mendeleev, had constructed in the 1890s seemed satisfactorily explained on this same basis of invisible, theoretical mechanisms of charged particles.

In this and in other ways, theories burgeoned and things got more and more complicated. For instance, a controversy had raged, since the days of Newton and Huygens, as to whether light consists of particles or waves. The problem with particles is that it seems inconceivable that particles can create interference patterns in the way that Thomas Young had demonstrated in 1801 with a beam of light split in two and then recombined, as in his famous two-slit experiment. However, other experiments with light, such as the photoelectric effect in which electrons are ejected from the surface of a metal suggested that light consisted of particles. Reverting to Newton's corpuscular theory of light, Einstein explained this photoelectric effect as due to a kind of 'bombardment' by light-corpuscles, which he called 'photons'.

But why did these particles strike with wave-like characteristics? The answer, it seemed, to Einstein and his follower, Louis de Broglie, was provided by a forcing together of the intuitively incommensurable wave and particle theories. And so the photon was conceived to be a 'wave particle'. The fact that an entity with such opposite characteristics is inconceivable was dismissed as inconsequential, so physicists went ahead with that concept, on the grounds that it was a 'fact of nature', regardless of how intuitively unimaginable it might be that these photons had the dual nature of particles and waves. This rejection of commonsense logic and intuition was graced with the name of a principle, the principle of so-called 'complementarity'. It created the fashion, from then on, of allowing a special dispensation for physicists to think and teach in this completely nonsensical way.

As de Broglie conceived it, this 'wave-particle duality' of matter extends throughout the sub-physical world, being associated not only with Einstein's 'photons' but with the motions of particles of any sort, protons, electrons or whatever. De Broglie thought of these waves as 'pilot waves'. In atoms, they form stationary, or standing waves, in harmonic patterns around the nucleus at distances from the centre where the beginning and end of the wave-cycle are perfectly in phase. This concept of the underlying standing-wave was further developed by Erwin SchrÃ¶dinger who, in January of 1926, introduced into theoretical physics a wave-equation that satisfactorily described all the quantum aspects of physical systems. Individual states, or manifestations of these physical systems were then regarded as solutions of this underlying 'wave-function' involving the superposition of harmonic states.

Later, in the summer of 1926, Max Born subjected SchrÃ¶dinger's wave-function to a probability-statistical interpretation, describing it not as a continuous 'wave' but as a pure probability amplitude. In this interpretation, exactness of position and motion of elementary particles had disappeared at the quantum level. All that was left were pure wave-like 'probability functions'. The traditional notion of a 'particle' as a hard material entity was thus fast disappearing. It was to be replaced by the concept of a real physical state brought about by the 'collapsing' of a continuous, all-pervading and underlying 'wave-function' â€” by the mere fact of observing it. In this way, quantum physics slid further and further into the mystery we now commonly associate with it.

In 1927, Heisenberg made another discovery. This was what he called his 'principle of uncertainty'. Observing the state of a fundamental particle requires an energy-interaction with that particle, which alters its state. So the only way to determine the 'exact state' of the particle would be not to observe or detect it in any way. This means that so far as observation is concerned, the existence and state of particles on the ultimate quantum level is impossible to describe. In that case, how can these ultimate 'particles' be said to exist? Heisenberg concluded that they can't, so that particles as such do not exist at the ultimate microphysical level.

In fact, with this principle of uncertainty (or 'indeterminacy principle' as it is sometimes called) Heisenberg had plumbed the depths of all knowledge of our world, right down to its ultimate epistemological elements. In the sort of theoretical physics which sees itself as seeking to describe realities "behind the veil of perception" things were getting fraught, to say the very least. Theories began to burgeon at an exponential rate. Anyway, as already stated, to produce a chronicle of the precise details of the historical slide into the current mystique is not our concern here. What does concern us is that despite the undoubted practical efficacy of these developments for industrial, commercial and military purposes, they are all based, like 'light in vacuo' and in vacuo 'gravitational force', on concepts of things and processes that are altogether unseen and unsensed, things existing â€” if that is the word â€” in realms apart from ordinary reality. Moreover, these 'hidden mechanisms' are conceived mostly by analogy with our experiences of purely mechanical causes and effects, such as pushes and pulls, and processes like that of the conduction of sound in air or in water. But these are as inappropriate for understanding processes at the quantum level as it would be, at the level of information theory, to try to analyse a video-screen picture of a horse in terms of a knowledge of nothing but horses.

The situation is further complicated by a proliferation of meta-theories as to how this world of remote theoretical 'realities' and that of ordinary observational reality interrelate, a continuing failure to establish which goes a long way towards answering the question we started with: 'What is wrong with Modern Science?' There can be little doubt that this modern fashion of producing theories for theory's sake started with Bohr. It is reputed that a student of Bohr's, on presenting his teacher with a theoretical suggestion, told Bohr that he was worried that it might seem absurd. It is reported that Bohr replied: 'My worry is whether it may be absurd enough!' In this way, with the introduction of more and more counter-intuitive, underlying 'realities' such as those of 'wave-particles', 'complementarity', holes in a vacuum ('black holes') and other complete absurdities, contemporary physics has now very plainly advanced beyond the pale, so far as any prospect of common understanding is concerned.

Was this assault on commonsense ever really necessary? The end-aim of all those theories was to rationalise what is actually observed on the level of ordinary reality. This, as ever, remains the primary, or 'square-one' concern of any true science. But were there no explanations of these phenomena other than by way of such metaphysical excursions? Indeed there were, as may now be demonstrated.

IV.2. The ahistorical route

In the first place, we have shown that for information to be information, its ultimate elements have to be discrete and indeterminate. This is what was confirmed by the work of Heisenberg and Planck. Next, we have demonstrated that angular momentum, as a measure of visible motion, is perfectly sufficient in itself to replicate Bohr's model of the hydrogen atom without the addition of any exotic concepts of invisible 'charges' and their associated invisible 'fields'. Moreover, it has been shown that hidden 'corpuscles' and 'waves' â€” far less 'wave-particles' â€” are neither necessary nor appropriate as concepts of how light is supposed to 'travel', and that the ultimately probabilistic nature of quantum occurrences as projected by SchrÃ¶dinger is no more than a logical requirement of the informational package. So what, in these alternative, purely observational terms, explains the bar-code-like structure of spectra that is represented by the numerological formula of Balmer and Rydberg? The answer to that is as follows.

The parameter that is measured in our observations of spectra is essentially that of time â€” or, rather, the inverse of time, which is frequency, as displayed in those experiments on light interference which reveal a resonant character in light-interactions. Now we already have an observational formula for time. This is our elementary Pythagorean facsimile of the Einsteinian formula (3), in the above section I.1, viz.:

$$t_p = t_h \sqrt{1 - (v^2/c^2)} \tag{3}$$

This, of course, is for time as it is observed among objects on the macrophenomenal level. Let us now see what happens to this formula when its time-variables are reduced to measures of quantum magnitude.

For instance, let $t$ in this formula be some proper fraction $1/N$ of some quantum period such that for $N = 1$, $t$ represents the coefficient of the whole series. Multiples $n$ of this quantum-period $t/N$ are then integers, as also are the submultiples $N$. In mathematical terms the formula is now Diophantine, meaning that all its multiples and divisors are whole numbers â€” as in the mathematics of Diophantus of Alexandria, whose books on the mathematics of rational numbers were written some time between the 2nd century BC and the 3rd century AD.

So here is that same time formula expressed in basic quantum form:

$$\frac{nt}{N} = \frac{t}{N}\sqrt{1 - (v^2/c^2)} \tag{6}$$

Now we know that the standard formula for potential energy (i.e., twice the kinetic energy $K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$) is $e = mv^2$. This means that we can substitute $e/m$ for $v^2$ in (6), which produces

$$\frac{nt}{N} = \frac{t}{N}\sqrt{1 - (e/mc^2)} \tag{7}$$

Squaring the formula to remove the square root and dividing by the $t$ that is common to both sides of the equation then gives

$$\left(\frac{n}{N}\right)^2 = \frac{(1/N)^2}{1 - (e/mc^2)} \tag{8}$$

Expressing this equation in terms of $N^2e$, we have

$$N^2e = N^2mc^2\left[1 - \frac{1}{n^2}\right] \tag{9}$$

Next, dividing this formula throughout by Planck's constant $h$ expresses it in terms of frequency, $\nu$, viz.:

$$\nu = \frac{N^2mc^2}{h}\left[1 - \frac{1}{n^2}\right] \tag{10}$$

In this way we arrive at a series of frequency levels of the bar-code kind that Balmer and Rydberg sought, by their trial and error method, to fit their observations of line-spectra. In fact, if we include in this series, intermediate values of $n$, taking $n_1$ and $n_2$ as the fixed and running terms, respectively, we obtain a precise facsimile of the Balmer-Rydberg formula, viz.,

$$\nu = \frac{N^2mc^2}{h}\left[\frac{1}{n_1^2} - \frac{1}{n_2^2}\right] \tag{11}$$

(compare formula (5) on page 21). The value of the coefficient $\frac{mc^2}{h}$ cannot, of course, be deduced in this way but has to be measured empirically. Its measured value is that of what spectroscopists call the Rydberg frequency-constant $cR$. With this textbook value substituted for $\frac{mc^2}{h}$ in (10), our deductive formula fits the Balmer-Rydberg formula exactly.

What, then, may we glean from this result? First, the reader may have noted the appearance in these equations of the famous $e = mc^2$ formula for mass-energy inter-conversion, as in orthodox relativity. There has been no special assumption involved in this deduction. It is purely a product of dimensional syllogistic as applied to standard definitions of the quantities involved. Equation (9) deals with measures on the ultimate microscale. On the macroscale, the $N$s in this equation lose their significance and cancel. Also the $n$s become so large as to approach infinity, which leaves us with the generalised macroscale formula

$$e = mc^2 \tag{12}$$

that is familiar to students of Einsteinian relativity.

Something else we glean is that in casting around for their basic pattern of numbers to fit their observations of spectral lines, the spectroscopists, Balmer, et al, need really have looked no further than the Einsteinian time-formula. But of course, that formula had not appeared on the scene at the time. Balmer died in 1898, whereas Einstein's formula was not published until 1905; and Johannes Rydberg survived no more than fourteen years beyond that. However, one may speculate as to whether, but for this anachronism, Balmer and Rydberg might have latched onto this Einsteinian time-formula as providing a template for the pattern of numbers they were seeking.

Undoubtedly, however, any such discovery would have been inhibited by the altogether esoteric and 'intellectual' nature of Einstein's mathematical theory in the context of which his formula was presented, with all its obligatory metaphysic as to how light was supposed to behave in the vacuum. This would surely have obscured the formula's value as a straightforward, non-theoretical explanation of spectra deduced from nothing more than measurements made by such observers as RÃ¶mer as to how time-processes behave with changing distance in pure observation.

V. Objective motion and time-dilation in orbiting bodies

Let us remind ourselves at this stage of the objective of our observer-based approach to physics. This is, wherever possible, to redirect our attentions away from the mystical world of theory and re-focus it on the real world of direct sensory and instrumental observation. In the previous section we have seen how this can be done simply by applying certain logical rules of numbers to our original time-dilation formula. This produces the basic formula for the spectrum as it applies to observation directly, that is, by circumventing the whole history of 'electrodynamical' explanation of that phenomenon.

This same direct logical route that has been driven through the theoretical thicket in the case of electrodynamics can also be extended to 'gravity'. For instance, in observational terms we already have our formula for orbital angular momentum, viz:

$$L = mvr = \frac{GmM}{v} \tag{4}$$

Now although this appears identical to the standard Newtonian formula there are, in fact, two vital differences. Not only is $G$ in the formula not a constant (see above, Ch. III) but also, like all observational velocities, the velocity $v$ is non-Newtonian â€” that is, it is the same relativistic $v$, limited to $c$, that appears in our time-dilation formula, namely:

$$t_p = t_h \sqrt{1 - (v^2/c^2)} \tag{3}$$

That being the case, from (4) we have

$$v^2 = \frac{GM}{r} \tag{13}$$

and substituting this equivalent expression for $v^2$ in (3) then produces

$$t_p = t_h \sqrt{1 - (GM/rc^2)} \tag{14}$$

In this simple and straightforward way, we move directly from a time-dilation formula for rectilinear motion, as expressed in (3), to a time-dilation formula for orbital motion as expressed in (14). As can be seen, this is without any reference whatsoever to the usual theoretical rationale of 'gravitation', etc. The substitution of $GM/r$ for $v^2$ in (13) expresses time-dilation purely syllogistically in terms of potential energy. This supports our reasoning in Chapter III that Newton's 'gravitational' hypothesis is redundant.

Unlike the classical 'gravitational potential' of Newton, the sign of the potential energy in (14) is positive. That is to say, the zero of reference for that angular momentum potential, hence the objective zero of time-dilation, lies at the greatest distance (the largest measure of $r$) from the central mass $M$, in deep space, far from the presence of massive bodies. This is in direct contrast to the classical so-called 'gravitational' potential, whose zero lies at the centre of radius â€” that is, nearest to the centre of the mass $M$. Formula (14) therefore predicts that in the case of simple circular orbits there will be a time-dilation, hence a slowing of clocks in satellites as they orbit closer to the central mass. As it stands, this simple formula applies only to ideally circular orbits or for orbits of unit eccentricity (that is, a straight up-and-down motion of a body with respect to $M$). The latter is confirmed by the Pound-Rebka experiment, in which clocks placed at different levels in a lift-shaft were found to run slower as they were placed lower in the shaft.Â¹ In the case of circular orbits, the slowing of clocks is verified by engineers and scientists involved with satellite systems such as that of the American Global Positioning System (GPS) and the Russian equivalent, GLONASS. Discussions with these experts have disclosed that clocks on these satellites run quicker than clocks on the earth's surface, albeit slower than clocks further out in space, which is direct qualitative confirmation of our formula (14).

For instance, for an orbital velocity $v = 0$ in equation (3), the time-dilation is zero. That same value of zero for $v$ in equation (13) makes the orbit radius $r$ infinite, which means that the datum-zero of the scale of potential energies and time-dilations is that of a clock in deep space.Â² With respect to that deep-space time-standard (let's call it DST), clocks orbiting at finite distances $r$ from massive bodies lose time. For example, terrestrial clocks in solar orbit lose 426,160 nanoseconds every twenty-four hours DST, while the moon in terrestrial orbit loses just over 498 nanoseconds DST â€” as would any clock orbiting that distance from earth. In the case of an earth satellite, from the average data supplied by the GPS engineers the formula predicts, for a satellite in circular orbit of radius $6.372828 \times 10^6$ metres measured from the earth's centre, a loss of 7216.4 nanoseconds per 24 hrs DST. By that same formula, a clock orbiting a mass equivalent to that of earth at a distance $r$ equivalent to that of the earth's surface radius would lose 30075.66 nanoseconds per 24 hrs DST.

But of course, the clocks that register earth time (GMT) are not orbiting in that way, which would be at an earth-orbiting velocity of 7,910 metres/second. Being earth-bound, the fastest those clocks may travel is at the earth's equatorial spin-speed of 464.74 metres per second. Moreover, $G$ in our formula has its standard value only for bodies in free orbits.Â³ Nevertheless, taking the free-orbiting time-standard as approximate to GMT we have the result that as measured from earth the satellite gains 22859.26 nanoseconds per day, which is approximately 60% of the reported average value of 38,700 nanoseconds gained by those satellites as measured from the ground. VI.1 The 'EPR' Controversy

In the so-called 'Copenhagen' interpretation of quantum physics, after Bohr, a particle and its observation form a single angular momentum system. This wholeness of nature lies in its principles of overall conservation, as applied to measures of angular momentum, action, and so on, according to which these quantities can neither emerge from nothing nor lapse into nothing. However, while it is true to say that these quantities are 'universally' conserved, we must remember that 'universal' has two meanings. For instance, one may say that angular momentum is conserved in every action that takes place, so that when a pair of atoms interact, the one atom loses a quantum of angular momentum and the other gains precisely that same amount. In that case, in the system involving just those two atoms, the angular momentum is absolutely conserved: And if this is the case with every other atomic interaction that takes place, then one might say that angular momentum is conserved 'universally' according to one meaning of that word, namely, 'applicable to all cases'. However, that is not the same as saying that the angular momentum is conserved simultaneously between all atoms everywhere, according to the other definition of 'universal', meaning 'present or occurring everywhere, all at once'. If that were the case, then for one atom to interact with another would be to interact with all atoms everywhere. To move one thing would be to move everything, the consequence of which would be an Eleatic stasis.Â§

This distinction is subtle but significant. In saying that angular momentum is conserved 'universally' we do not mean that it is conserved 'monolithically', or everywhere at the same instant. We mean that angular momentum is conserved in every single case of atomic interaction, which is not quite the same thing (such being the nuances of the language we are constrained to use).

The same goes for the wholeness of a hologram, all parts of which are connected individually without necessarily being seen as a whole. However, in a hologram as ordinarily conceived, there is an overall design to the system, hence a designer, an overseer, a creator or 'presiding intelligence' which is outside the system, (a Deus ex Logica rather than a Deus ex Machina). Now whether there is the same sort of overall designer and 'presiding intelligence' in the World at large is a question that, in seminars on this subject, has been raised in that same uninhibitedly philosophical and logical, non-academic manner that has been described in the Preface. Some feel that the notion of an overall cosmic will and intelligence is inconsistent with any idea of moral freedom and autonomy. Others prefer the idea of a bifurcation of intelligences into good and evil, personified in Christian theology as 'God versus Satan'. Others again prefer to think of these evaluations and personifications as no more than conventionalised reflections of human likes and dislikes. There is, they say, no overall presiding intelligence but merely a conflict of wills and intentions between multifarious agencies, in a Darwinian 'survival-of-the-fittest' scenario.

But be that as it may, the revolutionary trends in modern physics, away from mechanistic, or atomic, ways of thinking towards these more holistic and relativistic, information-based modes puts an altogether different complexion on things. It places these humane, moral and spiritual issues â€” or, rather, replaces them â€” on a different footing from that of those long eras when human values remained in conflict with mechanistic science, the conflict which has undoubtedly led to the morally confused society we have today. With that old source of antagonism between 'mental' and 'material' finally removed in favour of a commonsense-logical merger of science and the humanities, perhaps the current wildest excesses of irrationality in both sectors may be curbed.

We end this section by saying that although it is the aim of this present small volume to explore the logical implications of the holistic paradigm as far as logic takes us, we should remember that, as has well been said, there are truths that without being contrary to logic are nevertheless beyond logic. As Hamlet says, in Shakespeare's play: 'There are more things 'twixt heaven and earth, Horatio, than are dreamed of in your philosophy.'

Perhaps, then, the best we can expect of even the most meticulously logical argument, is that although logic can never be taken as an oracle of truth, at least it is the only thing that can be safely relied upon to eliminate error. And what else but to eliminate error can be the common interest of society in all sectors of natural science and human reason?

IX. Theological and Social Implications

'God does not play dice', said Einstein, to which his friend, Max Planck, replied, 'Albert, you should stop telling God what he should and shouldn't do.'

What 'God' does or does not do is something we have no possible way of knowing, not least because we have no solid definition of what 'God' means. Even professed atheists fall foul of this fallacy. In a lesson on Liberal Studies at a Technical College, I once asked the students, 'How many atheists are there in this class?' All the hands went up. 'What does atheism mean?' I asked them. 'It means, they replied, 'that we don't believe in God'. 'Describe to me, then, I said, 'the God you don't believe in,' The answers they gave were completely bizarre. Descriptions ranged from that of a Zeus-like figure, sitting on a cloud, dispensing punishments with strokes of lightning, to that of some kind of 'Ghost' floating around spying on us and knowing what we are all thinking and doing. Not the least strange among these responses was that of the student who said, 'I'm not going to believe in God. F*** him!'

I then wrote on the board, I don't believe in Wuggies. 'Do you agree with that?' I asked. 'Well, how do we know,' said a spokesman, 'unless we know what a "Wuggie" is?' 'Precisely!' I replied, and left them to think about it.

Evidently, to declare either that one believes or does not believe in X is nonsense, unless one can know precisely what 'X' stands for, and since one can never know precisely what 'God' stands for, then what possible sense can there be in saying that one either believes or disbelieves in such a being? According to the Oxford English Dictionary, 'God' is the 'Supreme being, Creator and Ruler of the Universe.' But if, as we have reasoned in previous chapters, there is no such thing as a 'universe' but only a pluriverse or polyverse of independent, free-acting and often conflicting individual agencies, then what is there for anything to be a 'Supreme Creator' of and 'Ruler over'? If, as Voltaire put it, 'God is present everywhere... but everywhere where there is something, not where there is not', then insofar as many of these 'somethings' are separate and distinct, free agents with wills and intentions of their own, what sort of logically coherent idea of such a 'Supreme Being' can we sensibly construct?

The fact, then, of there being so many conflicting wills and intentions seems to contradict any notion of an Overall Presiding Will and Intention directing and coordinating the actions of these multifarious free agents. However, there are, in nature, organisms that share a common purpose. Ants in a colony and bees in hive, as well as flocks of birds and shoals of fish, are sometimes observed by naturalists to act together as one in the evident interests of group survival. Observing this, we might judge that there is some altogether inexplicable 'telepathic' contact between these individual organisms. Yet nothing has ever been observed to travel in between these individuals to make a connection.

On the other hand, it is plain that organisms communicate by physically detectable means. Ants lay and follow chemical trails, birds and other animals communicate both behaviourally and vocally, land-animals do so with vibrations in air and the ground, as do aquatic creatures in water. In all these cases, the means of communication is physically detectable and identifiable, so that there is no mystery to contemplate on that score. Consider, for instance, an army general urging his men into battle. In this case, a single individual, by visual and audio information may be the source of concerted action on the part of many other individuals, so that the will of one becomes the will of many. All this is obvious; just plain commonsense. Where the mystery arises is when we try to explain the goal-directedness of these concerted actions in classical mechanistic terms. In the context of our non-mechanistic, information-based physics, all that mystery disappears completely. This is because, by its very nature, information is goal-directed.Â¶

Volitional autonomy, then, among individuals does not necessarily entail conflict. Wills and intentions may be concerted in the manner described, that is, by some coordinator, such as an orchestra conductor, an orator like Winston Churchill or Adolph Hitler, or by behavioural exemplars such as a Jesus or a Mahomet. From where, it may be asked, do these sorts of people obtain their power? Not from some supernatural 'spiritual' influence floating around in the 'imaginary spaces' that Voltaire talks about. From where, then? Where else but from other individuals, from mothers, fathers, teachers and other social influencers as one advances from birth into adulthood â€” from all the information one imbibes from these and other sources. Not least among these sources is nature in the round, experiences of which an individual may contemplate, uniquely in his or her own right aside from any social conditioning. Lone scientists and explorers may discover things that do not fit any traditional category of description or anticipation â€” such as, for instance, Copernicus's heretical discovery that it was the sun, not the earth, that was the centre of motion of the whole planetary system. Such discoveries involve a continual process of adjustment of our conventional idea-systems so as to keep them in accord with nature, in what is customarily called 'the search for natural truth and reality'. To think the opposite, that natural truth and reality must conform to our conventions, is plainly absurd.

IX.1 'There is no truth but only opinion'

But of course, in this sophistic society of ours there are many who do not believe in objective truth and reality. In another Liberal Studies lesson, I wrote on the board: 'There is no such thing as truth but only opinion,' I then asked class after class if they thought the statement was true. Three hundred or so students, in turn â€” barring none â€” answered that it was. I then asked these classes whether the answer they had given was really true or only their opinion. It is not too much to say that I was astounded at how many, quizzed in that way, were unaware of the contradiction involved in insisting that the statement was true.

As a matter of plain logic, to claim it as true that there is no such thing as truth is a contradiction and therefore necessarily false. And, logically, the negative of a statement that is necessarily false is necessarily true. This does not mean that we have to know what 'The Truth' is. All we can be sure of is that there is truth â€” simply because to deny it is a contradiction.

The same goes for reality. If nothing was real, then there could be nothing which, in comparison, could be identified as illusion. But there obviously is illusion. Every time we find that we are deluded or fooled into thinking or perceiving something that turns out to be false, we are discovering something more real. Seeking for the 'Ultimate Reality' may well seem an endless metaphysical quest, to be scorned by sophisticates of modern science and philosophy. However, for these people to assume that there is no such reality is to imply that reality and illusion are objectively undifferentiable, a possibility which is negated by the very existence of science and philosophy.

Admittedly, then, while it might be too much to describe either science or philosophy as a 'search for truth', in both cases, sophistry is avoided by a dictum such as, 'Although I don't know what "The Truth" is, I sure as hell know what is not!' In this way, truth and reality are sufficiently clearly and objectively defined to ensure the continued need for science and philosophy in human society â€” in opposition, if necessary, to the demands of social convention. This means that there are always individuals who will refuse to go along with convention â€” 'along with the herd', as most others do. Social conformity for its own sake can therefore never have been the overriding concern of human society. Indeed, in so many instances, pressures towards conformity have proved antagonistic to a community's very survival. Due to an over-concern with social conformity, whole cultures may stultify and die. (One thinks, for instance, of the Medieval Church in the face of nascent science, of Hitler's design for the Aryan race, Marxism in Russia... etc.) At the cutting edge, therefore, of all human evolution and survival on this planet, there has to be at least some provision for truth and reality to be pursued for their own sake, as ends-in-themselves. What provides for this continued need for adaptation to nature is the natural curiosity that is possessed by the higher orders of animals in proportion to their intelligence. In man, this curiosity, or drive for truth, often overrides all other considerations, and may run counter to all prospects of social acceptance and acclaim for the individual concerned. And what is certain is that without these asocial â€” and often antisocial â€” excursions on the parts of its individuals, human society could never have existed in the first place. For instance, what the Medieval Church regarded as the greatest heresy on the part of Galileo has turned out to have been essential for the evolution of our modern society, to the extent (ironically) that the Church which had threatened to liquidate him eventually had to 'forgive' him â€” posthumously, of course. One might say, therefore, that to have its proportion of 'heretics' is as essential to the health of humanity as a whole as for there to be at least some independently functioning individuals such as scouts in colonies of ants and bees.

IX.2. The objectivity of moral evaluations

And if this is the case with truth and reality, then what about the other verities? What about goodness and justice, for instance? Are these also, like truth and reality, ends to be pursued objectively in their own right, or are they no more than social conventions?

But, as we have already seen, without these focuses on ends beyond itself, society would naturally disintegrate. For it to survive by nothing but its own efforts would be like a drowning man trying to survive by tugging at his own hair. Besides, saying that these verities are no more than relative to each of us, as persons and societies, does not deny the objectivity of their existence. For instance, in the above section VIII.1 page 62 we read: When various observers look at a tower in the distance, every perspective of that tower is different. Yet all may concur, from a community of those perspectives... that the tower has, in itself, a height, breadth, depth and location. This enables us to describe the tower as having an objective existence independently of all the various views of it.

Can the same objectivity be claimed on behalf of evaluations such as Truth, Reality, Goodness, Justice and Beauty? In the context of our holistic way of thinking, the traditional, hard-and-fast, dualistic distinction between material and formal (concrete and abstract) no longer applies, which means that such perspectival objectivity as applies to things like towers and so on may also apply in the same way to those other evaluations. During what has been called the 'Golden Age of Greece', these sorts of evaluations, upon which their culture was based â€” and which still serve as a basis for modern cultures such as ours â€” were as objectively defined as any 'tower in the distance'. The sculpture of that era, for instance, in its expression of the perfection of the human body and the ideal of form and balance in its architecture and so on, provided for them a standard of beauty for future generations to emulate. Aristotle's Logic provided the criterion for discerning truth from mere opinion and delusion that still applies today. His science also provided the ideal of an objective or natural reality as a focus for critical observation and measure that led to modern science. In the notion of the Greek aretÃ© (virtue) we have the objective ideal of a moral Good as applied to individual behaviour, and in the overall balance of these things we have the ideal of personal and social justice.

However, not all cultures are based on these Greek standards. For instance, an African ideal of beauty may differ quite radically from that of the Greeks. In Asia the standards of perfection exemplified in the statues of the Buddha and in the architecture of the Buddhist temples are hardly comparable with those of the Greek gods and the Parthenon. Yet these cultures are never so alien to one another as to share no similarities whatsoever in their ideals, For instance, those ideals that proved essential to the Greek culture have their parallels in other cultures, even those that have evolved separately and independently. And the fact that all these cultural ideals are inter-interpretable gives promise, even if only potentially, of a global unification and objectifying of these ideals for the sake of mankind in general.

Does this potential for the objectifying of these ideals, then, make them real? The tower in the distance is real enough, although there had to be a time when it existed as no more than an idea in the minds of its architects and planners. The same can be said of any system such as that of the network of our modern institutions, like our transport and other systems, commercial and industrial. How could these things ever have 'evolved' without being preceded, in some way or another, by ideas and a logical processing of them in the minds of human individuals? To think that they could somehow 'emerge' on their own, purely mechanically, without individual human agency, is to embrace some kind of mythology.

In any case, are the ideals of beauty, goodness, justice and so on which are so essential to the creation and health of these institutions any less real than the piles of materials out of which they are constructed? Are they any less real for having once existed nowhere but in the minds of their founders? Insofar as these ideals point necessarily beyond us individually, in our lives and ordinary mundane concerns â€” even, in some cases, beyond our political institutions (as when these latter become corrupt, for instance, in the judgements of some of their members) they are, by definition, superhuman. However, as their consequences may attest, many of these ideals are illusory. But they cannot all be illusory, otherwise no society or community would ever be possible. Many of the ideals by which our social institutions are generally judged good or bad are therefore judged in accordance with criteria that do not emerge from within those institutions themselves. History shows that these criteria by which institutions are judged may be more recognisable to their individual members at the time than to the custodians of the social norms and standards. As is well known, this may be to the extent, sometimes, that these far-sighted individuals may be prepared to sacrifice their very lives in opposition to the political status quo, as Socrates did in taking poison rather than being forced to conform, and as Giordano Bruno did in choosing to be burned rather than renounce his support for the views of Copernicus.

IX.3 The goal-directedness of morality

To what ends or goals, then, do these ideals point, since it cannot be to the near-sighted here-and-now of comfortable conformity within a society? Do they signify the existence of some superhuman will and intention that prompts us to seek goals beyond those of purely personal pleasure and social survival?

Evidences of such motivation abound among organisms in general as in the sacrifices made by parents for their offspring. (Mechanistic explanations of this phenomenon as due to what has been called 'the selfish gene' sometimes ignore the fact that the forward-looking or, goal-seeking propensities imputed to these genes have no parallel whatsoever in the world that is described in terms of our traditional 'physical laws'.)

Is there some super-coordinated ideal, an ideal of all ideals, some super end-aim which lies beyond human comprehension? If so, then, at best, it is something which, in its entirety, can only be guessed at. And if it exists, to whatever extent it may, then it has to be something manifesting itself from within us â€” within all organisms, perhaps â€” not something hovering in space, either now or in some mystically imagined 'future'.

IX.4 The absolute necessity of faith and belief

However, things we may have no hope of ever comprehending are not, for that reason, necessarily non-existent. I may not know what is around that next corner or over that next horizon, but I know that there is something there, although I may never actually see it. Not to believe that would incapacitate me.

At every waking moment, therefore, faith and belief are an integral part of our ordinary perception and experience. To confine all assurances of reality to what we directly and presently sense, as George Berkeley sought to do in his dictum esse is percipi ('To be is to be perceived'), have proved untenable. Another inseparable part of our ordinary integrated perception is the knowledge that one's own consciousness and intelligence cannot be all there is. To assume that other intelligences are no more than figments of one's own imagination would be to court insanity.

In the same way, we have to believe in a residual class of intelligence in nature which, regardless of what we might call it, is superior not only to our own intelligence but also to that of mankind as a whole. As we have seen, if no-one in a society recognised this, then that society would become completely moribund. This does not mean that we have to know everything about that super intelligence in order to know that it exists. We may sensibly accept its existence along with the knowledge that whatever it may be in itself is altogether beyond our understanding. We may even believe in its benevolence in the face of a nature that is 'red in tooth and claw'. In this way, we may share the universal 'religion' which is common to all life-forms on this planet, as expressed in the well-worn saying: "Where there's life, there's hope'. This is what has been variously described as the 'will to survive', or the 'survival instinct'. Regardless of what we may call it, this behavioural 'optimism' in the face of all that is hostile to life, sustains all living things and without it they would just lie down and die or else have remained, from the beginning, as uniformly inert as pebbles.

Should we, then, deify these things which are incomprehensible to us and in which, at best, we can only believe? The plain fact is that, in very large numbers, throughout the whole of human history, people have done so and still do. Does this, then, make these idealistic projections objectively real? Obviously not, because we may believe in and deify just about anything. It cannot be that the mere fact that we can imagine something gives it objective existence. Beliefs, like all perspectives, are subject to rational analysis. This is not the same as saying that nothing exists unless it is proved by logical reason. It is well said that there are truths that are beyond but never contrary to reason. The same applies to all other ideals such as beauty, goodness and justice, none of which is exempt from rational evaluation in the interests of social weal and survival.

But, of course, there is always the question of evil. Over a drink in a pub, I once asked a professional psychiatrist whether he believed in a God. He replied, reflectively and with feeling, 'No, but my work has certainly made me believe in a Devil!' Was this just a wry statement or was it an incipient concession to religion? It was, in any event, a statement made by a working psychiatrist, not a theologian. Nevertheless, for anything that may be positively conceived, its negative is also implied. The one makes no sense without the other (e.g., wherever there's an up there is always a down). The Greek ideal of aretÃ© was that of the virtuous man in a virtuous society, this being the ideal of both social and individual goodness. Any mismatch between those two in the matter of virtue, due to default on the part of the one or the other, was therefore the opposite of good; and any deliberate intention in that negative direction on the part of either the individual or the society was evil. That, basically, is what the word 'evil' signifies today.

The same goes for every one of those other ideals that have been mentioned. The signature of evil is plainly the negative of corporate social and individual health and harmony, and the fact that so many of us judge the actions of others to be evil implies that we harbour at least an incipient belief in an integrated social/individual good. This belief, however narrowly defined in social terms, may be very deeply ingrained â€” perhaps innate, genetically 'wired-in', as it were). Taboos against incest, for instance, are arguably not mere conventions, since incest also transpires to be physically deleterious to the health and survival of a family line. We may find, therefore, that the actions of some people automatically disgust and dismay us â€” in some cases, physically sicken us. These automatic feelings, regardless of whether we admit or deny them, betoken our leanings towards this belief or that. And never was there anyone alive and sane who was not morally offended by anything at all. The question of whether, or to what extent, there is some kind of objective common factor of morality, that is to say, some evaluation to be found in humanity generally as to what is morally acceptable or offensive, is a question which cannot be answered by fiat. Philosophers throughout history, from Plato to Nietzsche, from Thomas Hobbes to Karl Marx, Jeremy Bentham and Mao Zedong, have sought to provide well-meant theoretical solutions for the good of society as they saw it, all too many of which, unfortunately, have proved disastrous in their implementation.

So how, if at all, can we objectify the 'power of evil'? It might help if we recognise that there are two fairly distinct forms of life. One of these is the sort of life which is self-sufficient, which evolves and survives in its own right. Organisms which thrive on the processing of pristine, inorganic matter by utilising energy from sources such as the sun, from volcanoes and so on, are the primary form of life. If life had evolved on no basis other than that, then that might be regarded as the epitome of 'goodness', of wholeness or health for that life form and even for life in general. On the other hand, there are life-forms whose existence has to be parasitic on life that is already there. In general, these parasitic, or predatory life-forms (of which we, the human species, are an example) provide the negating or opposing principle of life which, in our common language we indicate with words like 'bad', 'ill', 'evil' or some other linguistic variant with the same or similar meaning.

An example of this polarity is provided by the modern phenomenon of the INTERNET. In its inception, this was a perfect means of communication throughout the planet, in which any individual, anywhere on the globe, would be able to communicate with any other individual anywhere else. The situation presented was an ideal of that which was provided by the stoa in Athens, where all the citizens could be gathered within earshot of the speaker â€” somewhat like the provision of the soapbox for orators in Hyde Park. But what then happened with the INTERNET reveals what happens with life in general. Any flaw in the system is exploited by what might be described as 'the opposition'. When this system of human communication began it was potentially perfect. But no sooner was that system up and running than 'viruses', 'worms' and so on, began to appear which revealed that there were deficiencies in the system. These deficiencies were exploited simply because they were there. Viruses were developed because they 'fell into the cracks', as it were, of the system on the principle that, sooner or later, whatever can happen will. Arguably, these threats provide a service for the system in highlighting its deficiencies and in the correcting of which the health and strength of the system overall may be improved. In that case, what is 'good' for the system is for it, ultimately, to prevail against these attacks on it, and what is bad, or 'evil' is for it to succumb into extinction.

The 'triumph of evil', in these terms, would be, of course, for all such systems and organisms to disappear off the face of the earth â€” 'for the evil to overcome the good' in our customary language. And who is to say, other than in hindsight, what is good or bad for a society or for the individuals in it? What good, for instance, might have been created by people with evil intent, or what evils might have been created by those of good intent? ('The road to hell', it is said, 'is paved with good intentions'.) Philosophers have wrestled with these questions for millennia without coming up with any satisfactory answer.

IX.5 Faith and reason

However, one thing is certain. This is that the new holistic, information-based science does not preclude the possibility of such a remote end-aim as a world-morality in the way the old one did with its built-in classically 'scientific' scepticism and cynicism about the reality of anything beyond atoms and atomic mechanisms. The difference, as someone has put it, is between dealing with nature as a Machine or dealing with it as a Mind â€” or, at any rate something more in the category of 'mental' (i.e. informational) than 'mechanical'.

This radically different scientific approach to nature is socially ground-breaking. Not only does it leave room for religious faith but, in connecting scientific reason with religious faith, it also automatically connects religious faith with scientific reason. This closes the gap between science and religion as these are traditionally conceived. The advantage this offers is to curb those excesses of irrationality which characterise what are plainly the wildest, most dogmatic and dangerous of religious faiths. In fact, this (potentially) seamless connection between a rational science and a rational religion puts the two into the same logical category of anti-dogmatic natural philosophy. This should be as open-ended in its theories about nature at its microphysical boundaries as about nature on the macroscale. This philosophical open-endedness proffers the best antidote to the sort of dogmatism that presumes to know, absolutely and unshakably, 'the Mind of God' on all levels of human experience.

IX.6 Reason, the antidote to dogma

Now usually we associate the kind of 'God's-eye-view' dogmatism described above with priests and other advocates of the various religions. In fact, however, the worst of these dogmatists are, very often, scientists. For instance, what many scientists do in the name of 'science' is implicit religion, which is often all the more dogmatic for being undeclared and unexamined (witness, for instance, Einstein's above-quoted statement: 'God does not play dice'). The worst offenders in this way are those mathematicians and cosmologists who, in their minds, encompass everything within what they imagine is 'The Universe' and then go about describing the mathematical intricacies of this creation of theirs whilst remaining completely unaware that it resides nowhere but inside their own heads. One of the forms this scientific religion, or deism (called 'the God of the Scientists') usually takes is tacitly to presume that there is an overall, universal presence with which they can identify and relative to which everything happens everywhere all at the same time. This is an idea that has characterised classical science since its very inception and which militates, in the name of "Dissidence'â„¢ against any true understanding of modern relativistic and quantum physics. Along with this notion of a universal and absolute 'God's-eye-view', goes a belief in the existence of a universal 'present moment' in which time 'ticks away' metaphysically, at the same rate everywhere, regardless of what happens to clocks and other time processes in systems which move relatively to one another. This is as mystical and unsupported a belief, in science, as may be found among any 'unscientific' religious sect.

The upshot, then, of our connecting up of religious belief and logical reason should be to unify, to an altogether unprecedented extent, the sciences and the humanities. This is within a rational, open-ended and anti-dogmatic natural philosophy in which all our various human concerns are united in the way that only reason can achieve. It should also restore the missing link between the phalanx presented by self-interest groups such as politics, commerce and industry, on the one hand and, on the other hand, the sorts of concerns for the preservation of the environment and man's proper place in it that motivates more and more altruistic 'naturalist' groups. It offsets the terminal, built-in cynicism of classical mechanistic physics according to which the 'universe' is no more than a machine. Since there can be no morality in a machine, then a human society based on nothing but a 'mechanistic' ethos is consigned sooner or later to moral self-destruction. There are disturbing signs that this may already be the path of sheer irrationality on which our modern world-society is set. One cannot help noticing a definite 'lemming-like' tendency towards such destruction among the escalating suicide groups at this present time of writing as in the sort of lunatic 'logic' whereby A kills C,D,E... etc., including even himself, in order to spite B; also the escapism which makes so many young people nowadays prefer languishing in a realm of drug-induced hallucination to living in what they see as an irrational and nonsensical world â€” a world in which, if it were rational, they might otherwise be healthily and happily engaged. At this rate, without any true moral 'north' by which our modern society might set its ethical compass â€” other than the illusion presented by rich celebrities who are as confused as anyone (and perhaps more so than most) about morality and reason â€” what has been called 'the human race' may now have become a race between Reason and a dinosaur-like extinction.

X. Summary

The essential difference between modern science and traditional science is its radical, although not yet completed, Copernican-like shift away from thinking of nature in the way that an all-seeing presence might be presumed to see it towards thinking of it as a vast manifold of finite observational or relative, point-centred perspectives. What we call 'Science' is no mystically inspired 'God's-eye-view' of things. All our knowledge of the world is obtained via our finite senses and instrument-systems, and this knowledge is what we construct by means of our human faculty of reason, albeit in communication with other human observers and their instrument-systems. Science is therefore an essentially human undertaking, expressed in human language and processed by human logic and human mathematics. Losing sight of this fundamental principle has led classical physicists and cosmologists to assume that, somehow, they were studying 'the universe' when, in fact, they were doing no more than studying their own finite and fallible sensations and ideas.

Relativity disabuses us of that long-inculcated, 'God's-eye-view' presumption of classical science. For some people this loss can be traumatic. We are so used to thinking of the time by which we live our lives as the same everywhere. We never actually say it, but the tacit assumption is that the time by which we measure our living-times, between our birthdays, between our heartbeats, our cell-division rates and so on â€” one's age, in effect â€” is the same everywhere, not just throughout our immediate neighbourhood and planet but also throughout the stars and into the whole galactic and inter-galactic beyond.

But what warrant did we ever have for such an assumption? Time is what we measure with clocks. What else, indeed, can time possibly be? Normally, we don't think of these clock-rates as varying with motion, but RÃ¶mer showed that they do. He discovered that as clocks travel away from or towards us (in his case, the clock-movement being that of Jupiter's satellite) they are seen to lose or gain time in the ratio of one second for every three-hundred million metres. This affects all clocks in the same way, although, until RÃ¶mer, no-one had noticed this.

Einstein was the first to realise what this meant for physical measures in general. Time is one of the basic physical dimensions, and so are length and mass. Moreover, these measures are all inter-dependent in defining the existence of a physical object. So if time varies observationally, then so do those other dimensions of physical objects. This relativises our whole conception of matter and material objectivity. (Undoubtedly, this is why so many people have a phobia against accepting the theory of relativity, because they feel, deep down, that what they think of as this 'loss of Objectivity' in science would be traumatic.)

However, as we have seen, there is no real loss of objectivity implied by relativity. More serious as a barrier to accepting relativity is an amazing mistake made, most surprisingly, by Einstein himself. Having established, mathematically, the relativity of length, time and mass, he over-interpreted RÃ¶mer's length-time constant $c$ as 'the speed of light in space'. So light, which is, in itself, the information from which all our knowledge of space and time stems was made to travel in that very same space and time that is projected out of it. In other words, space-time was made to travel in itself, a completely circular and therefore fallacious assumption.

If only Einstein had settled for accepting $c$ in Bondi's way as a pure dimensional constant, modern science might have been disabused of its classical, God's-eye-view presumption of ubiquity and re-located, cleanly and clearly on a truly relativistic, observational basis.** The result would undoubtedly have been socially unsettling, as all revolutions inevitably are, but at least we would have known where we stand. We would have been relieved of all the tortuous, mind-bending jiggery-pokery that goes with 'relativity' as now commonly understood. 'Relative' would have simply meant what it always meant: 'relative to', or 'in relation to' as opposed to 'absolute', or 'self-sufficient'. The conceptual switch from absolutism to observationism made by Einstein's mentor, Mach, in the phenomenalist tradition of Berkeley, Hume and Kant, could then have been achieved at a stroke. Light (in its total spectral range from the deepest infra-red to the farthest ultra-violet and gamma) would have been observational information, nothing more nor less. Space would be what is projected out of that information, and so would time, with all other physical dimensions and measures following suit. Science would eventually have come 'down to earth' from its theoretical realms apart, to become re-united with commonsense and the humanities generally. The 'Great Machine' of classical physics would have disappeared and been replaced by an Informational System answering more to the description of a vast computer or hologram. The classical 'Universe' would have been replaced by a Manifold of interacting and intercommunicating agents, and any overall intelligence would have to be conceived as a superhuman Communicator that is directly involved in nature, not remote from it as in the deistic concept of a transcendental Machine-Designer. Any such superhuman agency would have to work in and through Man and his fellow organisms. In other words, if there is a God, then, as Voltaire wrote:â€ â€ 

> (He) is present everywhere... but everywhere where there is something, not where there is not. To be present in nothing seems to me a contradiction in terms, an absurdity... I will not set up the throne of God in imaginary spaces.
